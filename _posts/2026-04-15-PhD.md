April 15 (the Commitment Day) officially marked the end of my PhD application cycle. For Fall 2026 admission, I applied to PhD programs in both computer science and biomedical engineering. I started the process feeling completely lost. Along the way, I got incredibly lucky to have supportive professors, mentors, and friends who offered guidance. I also benefited so much from blog posts, spreadsheets, and application write-ups shared by people who had gone through the process and generously shared what they learned.

This post is my small attempt to do the same. I want to share from my perspective as a CS/BME PhD applicant, the resources I genuinely found helpful and things I wish I had known earlier. If you’re just starting out, I hope something here makes the process feel a little less overwhelming.

One quick note: PhD admissions are incredibly personal, and outcomes depend on many factors — funding situations, research fit, and timing all play a role in ways that are hard to generalize. There’s no single “right” way to approach the process, so take this simply as one perspective or one more data point as you navigate your own application journey.

## A Quick Background

I studied Computer Science and Mathematics at Vanderbilt University, with a minor in Data Science. I applied to PhD programs directly from undergrad without a master’s degree.

My research focus is primarily in computer-aided intervention and medical imaging, with around two years of research experience in these areas. As a result, most of the programs I applied to were related to these interests, including Computer Science, Biomedical Engineering, AI in Medicine, and Biomedical Data Science.

For more about my research background and projects, feel free to check out [my homepage]({{ site.baseurl }}/).


## Timeline of My Application Cycle

**December 1 / December 15 — Application Deadlines**  
Most of the programs I applied to had deadlines on December 1 or December 15. Many admissions offices slow down or close during the Thanksgiving break so don’t wait until the last minute to submit materials or reach out with questions.

**Mid-December — Early Interview Invitations**  
A couple of programs started sending out interview invitations in mid-December. One program I applied to announced on its website in late December that all interview invitations had already been sent out, so make sure to check department websites and application portals regularly.

**January – February — Interview Season**  
I received my first official interview invitation on January 8 and completed my last interview on February 2.

**Late January – Mid-February — Admission Offers**  
I received my first offer in late January and my final offer on February 14.

**March — Campus Visit Season**  
Most admitted-student visit weekends and campus visits took place in March.

**April 15 — Decision Deadline**  
Final commitment deadline for most PhD programs.


## Choosing a Program

- **Choose the lab as a key factor, not just the program ranking.**  
In my application process, I paid a lot of attention to research fit with potential labs and advisors, rather than focusing only on program or school rankings. While rankings and overall program environment still matter, the core of the PhD experience is research, which is largely shaped by your lab.


- **Apply to programs where there is more than one potential advisor match.**  
This was a piece of advice I received, and it felt especially relevant in fields that are more niche, such as mine. If a school only has one lab that fits your interests, you may lose flexibility later on — both in terms of collaboration opportunities and in case your advisor moves institutions or your interests evolve. Having multiple possible advisors within the same program gives you more stability and optionality.

- **If you are an international student, pay attention to international student admission patterns.**
In some cases, especially in programs with small cohorts, international students are admitted very infrequently. It is worth looking into a program’s historical data on international student admissions when deciding where to apply.


## Research Experience / Publications

**Do research before committing to a PhD.** I would strongly encourage having some research experience before deciding to apply for a PhD. A PhD is a long-term commitment to research, often for five or more years. Before deciding to apply, I think it is important to get some exposure to research and figure out whether you genuinely enjoy the process. Research can be exciting and rewarding, but it can also involve long periods of uncertainty, iteration, and things simply not working. Having firsthand experience helped me better understand what I was actually signing up for.

**Think carefully about research fit and motivation.** One of the most sincere pieces of advice I can give is to think carefully about whether you are genuinely interested in a research area before committing deeply to it. You do not need to know your exact dissertation topic when applying, but I do think it helps to have a coherent story about the kinds of problems you want to work on and why.

It is completely okay to apply to a field that is somewhat different from your previous research experience, but you should be prepared to explain the connection. Why this new direction? How does your past experience shape the way you think about the problems you now want to work on? Research fit matters a lot in PhD admissions, and my own results reflected this pretty clearly. Most of my offers came from labs that aligned closely with my previous research experience.

**Research experience is one of the strongest signals in admissions.** At least in my experience, admissions committees are not only evaluating grades or test scores. They are also trying to assess whether you have the potential to do independent research and contribute to solving open problems. One of the strongest indicators for this is the research you have already done: how deeply you engaged with it, what challenges you encountered, and how you think about problems.

**Publications matter less than research maturity.** Publications can certainly help, but I personally do not think they should be treated as the goal in itself. I found it more helpful to think of publications as simply one way of demonstrating your ability to do independent research. There are definitely other ways to show this as well.

More important, in my opinion, is whether you can clearly talk about the research you did, explain your contributions, discuss challenges honestly, and show how you think through research questions. Depth of involvement often matters more than simply accumulating projects or paper counts.


## Statement of Purpose (SoP) and Personal Statement

As an applicant, I heard very different opinions about how much SoPs and Personal Statements matter. Some people told me that committee-based programs care a lot about them, while others said they only play a relatively small role in admissions. I honestly do not know the “correct” answer, and it likely varies by program. But from my personal experience, I think these essays are worth spending some time thinking about.

For me, the SoP was an opportunity to show a side of my research experience that a CV alone cannot capture. Rather than simply repeating what is already on your resume (which I would strongly discourage), I found it more helpful to reflect on what I learned from each project, what challenges I encountered, and how my thinking evolved over time. In some ways, this also gives committees a sense of how independently you think as a researcher and how much ownership you took in your work.

The Personal Statement felt different. To me, it was more of an opportunity to show qualities beyond research: how you contribute to communities, work with others, overcome challenges, or what experiences shaped your perspective. Research matters, but so does being someone who can contribute positively to a lab, department, or broader academic community.

Beyond presenting your past experiences, both essays are also a chance to share what kinds of research questions interest you and where you hope to go next. You do not need to know your exact dissertation topic, but it helps to tell a coherent story about the research directions that genuinely interest you and why. If you are transitioning into a different research area from your prior experience, this is also an important place to explain the connection and justify why this shift makes sense for you.

One thing I found surprisingly difficult while writing was remembering that the person reading your application may know absolutely nothing about your field. As researchers, it is easy to become immersed in our own domain knowledge and unintentionally assume familiarity with technical ideas or specialized terminology. Try to write for a reader who may not know your exact research area and focus on communicating clearly rather than sounding overly technical.

Different schools may also look for different things in these essays, so I highly recommend carefully reading the prompts on department websites. Some programs are quite explicit about what they want to see. While I do not think you need to rewrite an entirely new SoP for every school, I personally found it worthwhile to tailor the final section to explain why a particular program felt like a strong fit — whether through specific labs, faculty, research opportunities, or unique aspects of the program.

I also remember feeling very lost while writing these essays. Looking through successful SoP examples from previous applicants helped me better understand ways people structured their stories and levels of technical detail. ## Interview Preparation & Common Questions

In most interviews, I was asked to prepare a 10–15 minute presentation on my research experience. It is very important to understand your research in depth and be prepared for detailed questions about your projects. One strategy that worked well for me was preparing a ~10-minute core presentation, with additional slides hidden for technical details. This made it easier to adjust the level of depth depending on the advisor’s questions.

I also tailored the order and emphasis of my projects depending on each faculty member’s research interests, which helped make conversations feel more natural and focused.

Below are some questions that came up in almost every interview:

**Why do you want a PhD?**  
Common pitfalls: “I want to take more courses.”  
In interviews, this question is often about whether you are motivated by research and prepared for long-term, open-ended problems. To me, a PhD is about learning how to define research problems and work on questions that do not yet have established solutions.

**What do you want to work on during your PhD?**  
You are not expected to know a very specific question you want to work on. More importantly, interviewers seem to look for whether you have a sense of what problems interest you, what feels still unsolved in the field, and how you think about the direction of the area.

**Why our school / why our lab?**  
This is about whether you understand what the lab is actually working on and can explain why you might be a good fit.

**Do you have any questions for me?**  
I found this to be a good opportunity to understand advisor style, lab culture, and expectations.


## Resources

I rarely see people talk about these resources online, but they gave me access to conversations and perspectives that are genuinely hard to get from department websites, rankings, or online searches alone. Most of the programs below are free or fully funded, and I would highly recommend applying if you are eligible.

### Application Support Programs

**[Princeton Pathways to Graduate School for Rising College Seniors](https://engineering.princeton.edu/graduate-studies/academic-pathways/prospective-graduate-students)**

I participated in this program which is designed for rising college seniors in STEM who are considering applying to graduate school (MS or PhD). Starting in September, it held biweekly sessions covering different parts of graduate school preparation, including recommendation letters, SOP/PS structure, CV building, and interview preparation.

The program also offered one-on-one meetings with personalized feedback on application materials. It helped clarify my expectations for each part of the process and gave me a better sense of how to present my application, which made the overall process feel much more manageable.

### Peer Mentorship Programs

Many PhD programs offer peer-to-peer mentorship that connects prospective applicants with a current PhD student in the program. I participated in **[MIT GAAP (Graduate Application Assistance Program)](https://oge.mit.edu/community-diversity/gaap/)** and **[Stanford DBDS Peer Program](https://dbds.stanford.edu/)**. Both were invaluable as talking to current students gave me a perspective on the program culture, day-to-day experience, and even the specific research directions of labs I was interested in that I simply couldn't have gotten from a website. I'd strongly recommend searching whether the programs you're applying to offer something similar.


### PhD Preview / Prospective Student Visit Programs

These programs invite and fund prospective students to visit campus in person. I participated in the [Stanford SERGE](https://engineering.stanford.edu/SERGE) program. The program included workshops on CV building, SOP/PS writing, and the overall PhD application process, as well as seminars where I had conversations with PhD students and admissions staff. These discussions helped me better understand the program culture and the admissions perspective.

Beyond the structured program, I also used the visit to reach out and have one-on-one conversations with PIs and PhD students from five different labs I was considering. I even had the opportunity to sit in on a few lab meetings. Overall, the visit gave me a much clearer sense of Stanford’s research environment, what kinds of students tend to thrive there, and—most importantly—what the labs I was interested in were actually working on.

## One Last Thing

The waiting can feel long. I still remember the moments of anxiety when I could not hear back from programs and found myself overthinking everything. Looking back now, what kept me going throughout the process was never the idea of a “perfect” outcome, but a curiosity-driven motivation, a sense of wondering how much I could contribute and change through research.

PhD admissions are ultimately a matching process between students, advisors, and programs. Results do not reflect a single measure of worth, but rather how well things align at a particular moment in time.

I wish you the best as you keep building toward something that feels meaningful to you.
